How They Track You

Every moment online, invisible systems are collecting data about your behavior. Here are the primary methods used to surveil your digital life.

Cookies & Third-Party Trackers

How it works: Small text files stored in your browser follow you across websites. Third-party cookies let advertisers track your journey across the entire web.

Example: You look at shoes on one site, then see shoe ads everywhere else you go online. That's third-party tracking.

Scale: The average webpage contains 10-40 third-party trackers. Popular sites can have 100+.

Device Fingerprinting

How it works: Your browser, screen resolution, installed fonts, timezone, language, and hardware create a unique "fingerprint" ‚Äî even without cookies.

Example: Even in "incognito mode," your device can be uniquely identified 99% of the time based on these characteristics.

Why it matters: You can't delete your fingerprint like you can delete cookies.

Location Tracking

How it works: GPS, WiFi networks, cell towers, and Bluetooth beacons constantly pinpoint your location ‚Äî often even when you've disabled location services.

Example: Google tracks Android users even with location history turned off. They were fined $391 million for this in 2022.

Who buys it: Retailers, insurance companies, hedge funds, and political campaigns all purchase location data to track your movements.

Social Media Pixels

How it works: Facebook, Twitter, and other platforms embed invisible tracking pixels on millions of websites. They track you even if you don't have an account.

Example: The Facebook pixel is on over 8.4 million websites. Every time you visit one, Facebook knows ‚Äî and builds a "shadow profile" even if you never signed up.

Data collected: Pages visited, products viewed, time spent, purchase behavior, and more.

Email Tracking Pixels

How it works: Invisible 1x1 pixel images in emails report back when you open them, where you are, what device you use, and how many times you read it.

Example: 70% of marketing emails contain tracking pixels. Many regular emails do too.

What they learn: When you're most active, if you're traveling, your typical routine, and which messages get your attention.

Smart Home Devices

How it works: Alexa, Google Home, smart TVs, and IoT devices constantly listen and watch, sending data back to servers.

Example: Amazon employees listened to thousands of Alexa recordings. Smart TVs track everything you watch and sell that data to advertisers.

Scale: Samsung's privacy policy states: "Please be aware that if your spoken words include personal information, that information will be captured and transmitted to a third party."
